Working in the DBU

You can find out about life in the DBU in more detail in the Welcome to the DBU induction pack.

DBU projects and ways of working

The DBU works across three areas:

  • Strategy and audit
  • Investment cases
  • Digital service delivery and programme management

Many projects have a similar structure and way of working to projects in other Business Units. Digital service delivery projects, however, have a different structure. These consist of three phases - discovery, alpha, and beta.

In all DBU projects, we follow agile methodology, specifically the scrum framework, structuring work in two-week 'sprints'. The ideas behind this are laid out in the agile manifesto and principles.

Planning a DBU project

The high-level strategic plan for a project is called the roadmap. It is used for communicating milestones and objectives, and includes actions being taken 'Now', 'Next' and 'Later'. The GOV.UK Notify roadmap gives an example of how they work.

More detailed planning is done through managing the backlog, a prioritised list of the work required, derived from the roadmap.

Backlogs are composed of stories, explanations of individual software features and how they will help users. These are grouped into epics, a group of stories related to a single deliverable.

Members of the DBU team

Any generalist consultant could be part of the DBU. Teams are generally around 3-10 people. Your role varies depending on the role you take on in the service team.

You can find out more about the current members of the DBU and what they've been up to on The PSC website.

Roles in a DBU team
The GDS Service Standards suggest the following team members are necessary:
  • Product manager
  • Service owner
  • Delivery manager
  • User researcher
  • Content designer
  • Designer - interaction designer, service designer, and/or graphic designer
  • Developer
Sometimes other team members are needed as well, including:
  • Performance analyst
  • Technical architect
  • DevOps engineer
  • Business analyst
  • Quality assurers and testers
  • Technical writers

In the DBU, junior team members are mostly likely to take on the roles of user researcher, content designer and business analyst.

Senior team members are likely to take on the product manager and delivery manager roles.

All team members are likely to play roles as quality assurers and testers.

Skills to learn and develop

Projects in the DBU require some generalist skills also used in other Business Units.

Numerical analysis is used to understand the context and content of projects, and communicate findings with stakeholders. Techniques include modelling, statistical analysis, and data visualisation.

Business analysis is used to define needs and understand potential solutions in an organisational context. Techniques and tools include MoSCoW rating, the Action Priority matrix, SWOT analyses, risk management, and the 5 Whys, among many others.

Many projects also present opportunities to learn some skills specific to the DBU.

User research is a core feature of user-centred (UX) design, helping to understand user needs, and test products to ensure they meet them. Techniques include interviews, observations and surveys. Insights can be gathered in a user needs databases, and used to create user journeys, empathy maps and user personas, among others.

Prototyping is used throughout alpha and beta stages of digital service design projects, to test and iterate ideas. More detail on the skills required, and guidance on how to develop them can be found on the GOV.UK Prototyping kit page.
